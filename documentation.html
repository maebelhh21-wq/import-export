Commercial Invoice

A commercial invoice is the first international document prepared by the exporter. It serves as a bill for goods, evidence of the transaction, and is used by Customs to classify merchandise and assess duties.

It must include: exporter/importer details, terms of sale, reason for export, product description, Harmonized Tariff Codes, country of origin, unit value, total value, package weight, and shipper’s signature.

Some countries may also require a consular invoice, purchased from their consulate and signed in their language. Exporters may also prepare a shipper’s letter of instructions to guide freight forwarders in handling the shipment.

Bill of Lading

A bill of lading is a contract between the exporter (owner of the goods) and the carrier. It serves as evidence that goods have been received and will be transported to the named destination.

Types: • Straight Bill of Lading (non-negotiable) • Negotiable Bill of Lading (transferable to third parties)

Inspection Certificate

Some importers require goods to be inspected by an independent company prior to shipment. An inspection certificate verifies that goods match the description provided.

Dock and Warehouse Receipt

This document transfers accountability once goods are delivered by a domestic carrier to the international carrier (ship/airplane). Freight forwarders often prepare this document.

Shipper’s Export Declaration

Used by the Department of Commerce for controlling exports and compiling statistics. Required for shipments over $2,500 or those requiring an export license.

Packing List

A document that details the contents of each package: type of package, net weight, legal weight, tare weight, gross weight, dimensions, and reference numbers. It helps Customs and buyers verify shipments.
